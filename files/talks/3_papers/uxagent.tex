\begin{abstract}

Usability testing is a fundamental research method that user experience (UX) researchers use to evaluate and iterate their new designs. But what about \textbf{evaluating and iterating the usability testing study design itself}?
Recent advances in Large Language Model-simulated Agent (\textbf{LLM Agent}) research inspired us to design \textbf{\projectname} to support UX researchers in evaluating and iterating their study design before they conduct the real human-subject study.
Our system features a Persona Generator module, an LLM Agent module, and a Universal Browser Connector module to automatically generate thousands of simulated users and to interactively test the target website.
The system also provides a Result Viewer Interface so that the UX researchers can easily review and analyze the generated qualitative (e.g., agents' post-study surveys) and quantitative data (e.g., agents' interaction logs), or even interview agents directly. 
Through a heuristic evaluation with 16 UX researchers, participants praised the innovation of our system but also expressed concerns about the future of LLM Agent usage in UX studies.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% \note{two challenge: usability testing design may be flawed -> insufficient, do not lead to good result -> study design is hard to identify before conducting the study. current practice is pilot study, ...}

Usability testing is a fundamental research method for evaluating the user experience (UX) of a new feature or a new webpage design~\cite{shawHandbookUsabilityTesting1996a}.
Typically, a UX researcher (or a UI/UX designer, product manager, etc.) leads the design and execution of a usability testing study: they start with a new feature or web design, and then they need to come up with a UX study design (e.g., A/B testing and the user tasks), recruit participants, execute the study, monitor, and analyze the data.
However, the current process of conducting usability testing often faces challenges, especially in the study design stage and the study execution stage~\cite{folstadAnalysisPracticalUsability2012, hertzumEvaluatorEffectChilling2003, kuangMergingResultsNo2022, norgaardWhatUsabilityEvaluators2006}.
In the study design stage, researchers often focus on collecting early feedback on new feature design to iterate the feature, but the study design itself is left unevaluated~\cite{shawHandbookUsabilityTesting1996a}.
Study design flaws that surface only during execution can lead to costly consequences. This is especially critical when working with underrepresented groups (e.g., non-binary shoppers), where participants are a limited and valuable resource, thus the findings may not be significant or insightful.
In such cases, a UX practitioner in a company may not even have a second chance to iterate the study design.
% If flaws of the study design are revealed until the study execution, the cost is substantial:
% participants are limited and valuable resources, especially when designing for the less-represented populations (e.g., older adults), so a UX practitioner in a company may not have a second chance to run the study or even little room for early iteration of the study design itself.


To alleviate this issue, researchers have practiced using \textbf{pilot study} sessions~\cite{decker-maurerMethodMadnessUsability2012} or \textbf{empathy-based research} methods~\cite{sarkelaMethodEmpathyBasedStories2020}:
Researchers often conduct a limited number of pilot study sessions before the actual study execution to test validity and uncover design flaws. However, these sessions are time-consuming and financially costly, since their data are not considered valid test data and require additional participant recruitment~\cite{decker-maurerMethodMadnessUsability2012}.
Researchers sometimes also apply an empathy-based research method, during which they attempt to put themselves in the shoes of their target users, especially when designing and studying for an underrepresented user population~\cite{sarkelaMethodEmpathyBasedStories2020}.
However, this method is inherently limited, as researchers may fail to accurately represent the target population and may unintentionally introduce bias \cite{whartonApplyingCognitiveWalkthroughs1992}. 
These limitations highlight the need for novel methods and systems that can offer early feedback on the study design as well as on the feature designs to support UX researchers.

With the advancement of Large-Language Models (LLMs), some researchers have prompted LLMs to act as autonomous agents (i.e., \textbf{LLM Agent}) that can perceive the environment and generate human-like actions to interact with the environment \cite{wangSurveyLargeLanguage2024}. 
For example, researchers have successfully empowered LLM Agents to simulate a group of 25 human agents living in a village \cite{parkGenerativeAgentsInteractive2023},
replicate participants in a social science study \cite{parkGenerativeAgentSimulations2024,schmidgallAgentClinicMultimodalAgent2024,leeApplicationsGPTPolitical,gurcanLLMAugmentedAgentBasedModelling2024}, act as patients and clinicians in a hospital \cite{liAgentHospitalSimulacrum2024}, and behave as programmers for a software development company \cite{qianChatDevCommunicativeAgents2024}.
% The most notable effort is that \citet{parkGenerativeAgentsInteractive2023} illustrated a simulated town with 25 LLM Agents, who can interact with each other and with the surrounding environment; this work argues that LLM Agents can generate ``believable'' human-like behaviors and reasonings, where ``believable'' implies that the agents ``appear to make decisions and act on their own volition'' like a real human~\cite{parkGenerativeAgentsInteractive2023, chen2025towards}.
The advances in LLM agent research inspire us that: if we can simulate diverse user behaviors and perspectives with an LLM agent as surrogates for real human-subject participants, then these surrogates can interact with real-world web environments to produce rich usability testing data.
Such data can support UX researchers to analyze and identify potential flaws in their study design before conducting real user studies, as well as offer UX researchers early feedback for feature designs. 
% This feedback has the potential to guide system development and shape hypotheses to be further validated in future studies with human participants.


Recently, a few LLM Agent-based systems (e.g., WebAgent~\cite{gurRealWorldWebAgentPlanning2023}, WebVoyager~\cite{heWebVoyagerBuildingEndtoEnd2024}, etc.) have explored simulating human interaction behaviors with web-based systems.
% For example, Voyager~\cite{heWebVoyagerBuildingEndtoEnd2024} can automatically accomplish web tasks within real-world websites like Google and Amazon.
For example, ReAct~\cite{yaoReActSynergizingReasoning2023} can generate both reasoning and action trace and simulate human customers on WebShop \cite{yaoWebShopScalableRealWorld2022}, a commonly used sandbox environment for e-commerce platforms.
Another example is Computer-Using Agent systems such as OpenAI Operator\footnote{\url{https://openai.com/index/introducing-operator/}} and Claude’s Computer-use API\footnote{\url{https://www.anthropic.com/news/3-5-models-and-computer-use}} that can complete tasks in computer environments.
However, these systems fall short of our use case: we need an LLM Agent to be able to operate with the dynamically changing web pages, to think (generate reasonings) as human users do, and to interact with the web page within a human-like responsive time interval.
The aforementioned LLM Agent systems (1) are restricted to a sandbox simulation instead of a real-world complex web system (e.g., ReAct~\cite{yaoReActSynergizingReasoning2023}), (2) rely only on complex and heavy reasoning architectures that hinder real-time responsiveness with the web (e.g., ~\cite{maLASERLLMAgent2024,heWebVoyagerBuildingEndtoEnd2024,gurRealWorldWebAgentPlanning2023}), (3) do not have the reasoning depth so the simulated behavior trace output is too simple in comparison to real-human's actions~\cite{sodhiStePStackedLLM2024}.
% he ability to operate outside sandboxed environments, responsiveness for real-time interaction, and human-like reasoning to simulate decision-making and produce analyzable qualitative insights.
These challenges call for a new LLM Agent architecture design (Fig.~\ref{fig:structure}) that can balance the reasoning capabilities with interaction speed when meeting complex and diverse real-world web systems.

In this work, we propose \textbf{\projectname} (Fig.~\ref{fig:system-arch}), a system that can generate LLM Agents as usability testing participants at scale and run simulated interactions with a given real-world web environment to collect simulated user behavioral data.
The UX researcher can define a demographic distribution and use the Persona Generator to generate thousands of personas for the LLM Agents, and then use the Universal Browser Connector module to interact with webpages via Chrome. 
Inspired by \citet{parkGenerativeAgentsInteractive2023} and the Dual Process Theory \cite{kahneman2011thinking,evansDualProcessTheoriesHigher2013,zhangLeveragingDualProcess2025,christakopoulouAgentsThinkingFast2024}, \projectname includes \textbf{an LLM Agent module }with a new LLM Agent architecture to have a two-loop structure to support both in-depth reasoning and real-time response.
Our \textbf{Universal Browser Connector modul}e allows LLM Agents to seamlessly parse and interpret real-world webpages while automatically executing real actions on the webpage without the need for manually pre-defined action spaces.
% \projectname produces a Simulation Replay and an Agent Interview Interface to allow UX researchers to view the LLM Agents' interactions with the webpage and conduct qualitative interviews in natural language conversation.
\projectname also provides a \textbf{Result Viewer Interface} that enables UX researchers to seamlessly review LLM agents’ interactions with web pages, examine post-study survey results, and conduct qualitative interviews with the agents.
Our position is that: \textbf{LLM agents are not meant to replace human participants in UX studies, but rather to help UX researchers to iteratively revise the study design, thus to be more responsible to human participants}.
LLM Agents can work together with UX researchers (human-AI collaboration~\cite{wangHumanAICollaborationData2019}) in a simulated pilot study manner to provide the desired early and immediate feedback for UX researchers to further iterate their design before testing with real human participants.


To evaluate our system, we conducted a user study with 16 UX researchers and asked them to analyze usability testing data (action trace, reasoning trace, and qualitative interview) of 20 LLM Agents interacting with a shopping website, as if they were the study owners.
Our study scenario is that: a UX researcher is tasked to design a usability testing study to gather feedback for a newly designed shopping website feature (product filter menu) and propose new designs for this website feature.
Our results suggest that the UX researcher participants were able to review and analyze the LLM-agent participants' data generated by \projectname, identify potential study design flaws, and come up with system feature improvement ideas. In comparison to the initial UX study design, they were satisfied with their revised study designs after analyzing the data, despite some criticizing that the LLM agent's generated actions were not what real users would do.
We conclude our paper by discussing how LLM agents may play an increasingly important role in the future of UX research.

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Challenges in Usability Testing}
Usability testing is a core component of UX research, used to evaluate how easily users can interact with a product to achieve their goals \cite{shawHandbookUsabilityTesting1996a,barnum2020usability, bastienUsabilityTestingReview2010, lewis2012usability}.
It involves observing real users as they navigate through tasks and providing valuable feedback on design effectiveness \cite{barnum2020usability}. 
This method helps refine products by identifying usability issues, measuring user satisfaction, and improving overall user experience. The key benefits of usability testing include validating design choices, avoiding internal biases, and ensuring the product meets user expectations.

However, UX researchers conducting usability testing on web designs have been facing multiple challenges in the \textbf{study design stage} and the \textbf{study execution stage}~\cite{folstadAnalysisPracticalUsability2012, hertzumEvaluatorEffectChilling2003, kuangMergingResultsNo2022, norgaardWhatUsabilityEvaluators2006}.  
During the study design stage, it is often difficult for researchers to craft appropriate study protocols and gather early feedback to iteratively improve the study design.  
To address this challenge, researchers have proposed guidelines and frameworks to support the design of usability testing, including methods for refining interview protocols~\cite{castillo2016preparing, shoozan2024application, roberts2020qualitative} and developing survey questionnaire items~\cite{rasinski2008designing, muller2015designing, muller2014survey, koelle2020social}.
To further streamline this process, recent work has begun to explore automated approaches for refining study protocols. For example, \citet{calvanoLeveragingLargeLanguage2025} investigated the use of Large Language Models (LLMs) to assist in generating components of usability studies, such as user tasks and evaluation criteria.  
To obtain early feedback on their designs, researchers typically rely on pilot studies, which, while valuable~\cite{hertzog2008considerations, truong2017pilot, van2001importance, oppenlaender2024state}, are time-consuming, costly, and limited in scale~\cite{Janaka2024, oppenlaender2021hardhats}.  
Despite these efforts, there remains a lack of efficient and scalable methods for UX researchers to evaluate and refine study designs before conducting full-scale studies with human participants.

For the study execution stages, a key challenge lies in the high cost and effort required to recruit enough qualified participants~\cite{Nebeling2013, Jane2024}. To mitigate these challenges, researchers have begun exploring the use of agents to simulate real human participants in usability testing~\cite{ren2014agent}. 
Prior work has introduced agents in various domains, including GUI testing~\cite{eskonenAutomatingGUITesting2020}, game environments~\cite{stahlkeArtificialPlayfulnessTool2019,fernandesAgentsAutomatedUser2021}, and accessibility testing~\cite{taebAXNavReplayingAccessibility2024, zhong2025screenaudit}. 
A particularly notable effort is SimUser~\cite{xiangSimUserGeneratingUsability2024}, which leveraged LLM Agents to simulate usability testing for mobile applications. In their approach, a user agent simulated human interaction, while an application agent represented the mobile app. These two agents interacted during the simulation to generate behavioral data. However, because the application under test was also simulated, their approach was limited in its ability to evaluate real-world interfaces and behaviors.
Despite growing interest in agent-based usability testing, the application of agents to real-world web design usability testing remains underexplored—largely due to the complexity of real web environments and the limitations of traditional web automation technologies.

\subsection{Simulation of Human Behavior with LLM and LLM Web Agents}

In the field of HCI, recent studies have demonstrated that LLMs possess the capability to simulate human-like behaviors, enabling research on personalized agent behavior. Unlike task-oriented agents, which are typically focused on achieving narrowly defined goals, \textbf{human agents} or \textbf{role-play agents}~\cite{chen2025towards} are designed with rich personas that encompass not only their roles and expertise but also their preferences, habits, and subjective viewpoints. 
For example, \citet{parkGenerativeAgentsInteractive2023} created a simulated town inhabited by 25 LLM Agents, each with a unique persona, and showed that these agents could exhibit believable and autonomous human-like interactions. \citet{parkGenerativeAgentSimulations2024} extended this line of work by simulating responses from 1,052 individuals in a social science survey, achieving an accuracy rate of 85\%.
Similarly, \citet{luPromptingNotAll2025} explored the use of LLM Agents to simulate virtual customers’ shopping behaviors on e-commerce platforms. 

LLM Agents have also been applied to study user attitudes toward privacy. For instance, \citet{zhangPrivacyLeakageOvershadowed2025,chenEmpathyBasedSandboxApproach2024} examined how agents with customizable personas can generate realistic search histories and profile behaviors to explore privacy concerns. In accessibility research, \citet{taebAXNavReplayingAccessibility2024} proposed AXNav, which uses LLMs to convert manual test instructions into replayable navigation videos for evaluating interface accessibility.
These emerging applications inform our approach: we aim to simulate realistic user behavior in web environments for usability testing, leveraging LLM Agents with diverse, persona-driven characteristics.

Recent advances in LLM-based Web Agents have significantly enhanced the ability of LLMs to assist with web-based tasks.
Early work like WebGPT~\cite{nakanoWebGPTBrowserassistedQuestionanswering2022} enabled GPT models to interact with search engines, improving question-answering performance. Subsequent systems, including WebVoyager~\cite{heWebVoyagerBuildingEndtoEnd2024}, LASER~\cite{maLASERLLMAgent2024}, and WebAgent~\cite{gurRealWorldWebAgentPlanning2023}, extended LLM capabilities to multimodal interaction, complex state space navigation, and long-horizon planning. Claude’s Computer-use API\footnote{\url{https://www.anthropic.com/news/3-5-models-and-computer-use}} further demonstrated precise, general-purpose control over user interfaces beyond web browsers.

Building on top of these advances, researchers have begun to explore the simulation of \textbf{human online behavior}. \citet{luPromptingNotAll2025} proposed a fine-tuning method to enhance model accuracy in predicting the next human action. OPERA~\cite{wangOPeRADatasetObservation2025} introduced a dataset of user online shopping behavior and benchmarked existing LLMs. Inspired by recent advancements in reasoning-enabled LLMs, Shop-R1~\cite{zhangShopR1RewardingLLMs2025} explored enhancing performance through RL. Still, existing work in the NLP field largely focuses on accuracy rather than mimicking human thinking processes. Since these approaches cannot capture qualitative aspects of user decision-making, key challenges in usability testing remain unresolved.

\subsection{Reasoning Architecture of LLM Agents}

Human cognition is often described as operating through two distinct systems: System 1 and System 2~\cite{kahneman2011thinking}.
System 1 governs fast, intuitive, emotional, and automatic responses, allowing humans to react quickly with minimal effort.
In contrast, System 2 is responsible for slower, more deliberate processes: it involves conscious reasoning, logical analysis, and problem-solving~\cite{kahneman2011thinking,evansDualProcessTheoriesHigher2013}.
Inspired by this dual-process theory, researchers have explored a variety of reasoning architectures in LLM Agent systems to balance speed and depth of thought.


For real-time responsiveness, many systems use System 1-style reasoning with single-prompt agents like OpenAI Operator\footnote{\url{https://openai.com/index/introducing-operator/}} and Claude’s Computer-use API\footnote{\url{https://www.anthropic.com/news/3-5-models-and-computer-use}}, which act on immediate inputs without deeper reflection. However, such agents often fail on complex tasks; for example, \citet{luPromptingNotAll2025} found System 1 alone insufficient for simulating realistic user behavior. To improve reasoning, researchers have introduced System 2-style approaches, such as chain-of-thought~\cite{weiChainofThoughtPromptingElicits2023}, ReAct~\cite{yaoReActSynergizingReasoning2023}, and reinforcement learning–based fine-tuning~\cite{deepseek-aiDeepSeekR1IncentivizingReasoning2025}.
These models excel in complex domains like programming and solving olympiad-level math problems~\cite{deepseek-aiDeepSeekR1IncentivizingReasoning2025,sunChallengingBoundariesReasoning2025}, but their high computational cost and latency~\cite{zhouSurveyEfficientInference2024,huaInteractiveSpeculativePlanning2024} limit their usability in real-time applications like interactive usability testing.

More recently, researchers have sought to integrate the strengths of both systems into unified reasoning architectures that can support real-time applications while preserving in-depth reasoning.
For instance, \citet{christakopoulouAgentsThinkingFast2024} introduced a talker-reasoner framework for a sleep coaching agent, simulating the interplay between fast reactions and deeper reflections.
Similarly, \citet{zhangLeveragingDualProcess2025} proposed DPT-Agent, which combines System 1 and System 2 thinking for efficient, simultaneous human-AI collaboration in game settings.
In particular, DPT-Agent uses System 2 to generate high-level instructions, while System 1 is used to translate those instructions into executable policy code for a finite-state machine.
However, extending this approach to the usability study scenario with web environments poses new challenges. 
Unlike structured game environments, web environments are dynamic and unpredictable -- each click can drastically change the context.
Agents need to continuously adapt their plans in response to newly observed content.
Building on these insights, we propose a two-loop architecture that combines fast, low-latency responses with reflective, high-level reasoning to simulate realistic, goal-directed user behavior in real-world web environments.

\subsection{LLM Agent Architecture for Usability Testing}


\subsubsection{Reasoning and Planning Architecture}
Inspired by the Dual Process Theory~\cite{kahneman2011thinking,evansDualProcessTheoriesHigher2013}, we designed the LLM Agent with a two-loop structure comprising a \textbf{Fast Loop} and a \textbf{Slow Loop} (Figure~\ref{fig:structure}). 
 % illustrates this architecture. 
The Fast Loop enables \textbf{rapid interactions with minimal latency}, allowing the agent to promptly respond to changes in the web environment and support real-time simulation.
In contrast, the Slow Loop performs \textbf{in-depth reasoning}, generating high-level insights and strategic guidance to ensure that the agent's behavior aligns with its intent and persona.
Each loop consists of modules that retrieve memory pieces from the \textbf{Memory Stream} (Section~\ref{sec:memory-stream}) and generate memory pieces to be stored in the Memory Stream.
The Perception Module produces \textit{observations}, the Planning Module generates \textit{plans}, the Action Module records \textit{actions}, and the Wondering and Reflection Modules create \textit{thoughts}.

The Fast Loop and Slow Loop operate \textbf{in parallel}, and share information via the \textbf{Memory Stream}.
For example, high-level reflections generated by the Slow Loop can influence subsequent planning and actions in the Fast Loop. 
This non-blocking, asynchronous architecture allows the agent to reason deeply while still maintaining responsiveness, making it well-suited for real-time simulations in complex web environments.

\paragraph{Perception Module}
\label{sec:perception-module}
% \note{mention why real, memory piece design}
% To allow the agent to adapt flexibly to different real-world environments by simply updating its perceptual inputs—without requiring task-specific tuning—we integrate a Perception module.
To allow the agent to observe and comprehend the outside environment, we designed the Perception Module.
The Perception Module observes the web environment and generates a series of memories describing the LLM Agent's observations.
Unlike simulated environments, our system interacts with real web pages, and the perception process is designed to mimic how a real human user might visually comprehend a web page.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:perception-demo}, the module systematically reads the web page from top to bottom.
The web page is then transformed into a text-form observation memory piece and stored in the Memory Stream.
These observation memories in the Memory Stream are then retrieved and used by other modules to inform subsequent planning, reasoning, and action.

\paragraph{Planning Module} 
\label{sec:planning-module}
To enable real-time decision-making, we designed the Planning Module to mimic human System 1–style rapid cognition by leveraging memory pieces generated by other modules.
This module continuously creates and updates short-term plans aligned with the agent’s intent and persona, based on relevant memories (such as observations and reflections) retrieved from the Memory Stream.
By relying on precomputed insights rather than performing explicit multi-step reasoning during execution, the Planning Module allows the agent to remain responsive while still benefiting from deeper, strategic thinking in the background.

The module mirrors human planning processes by breaking down tasks into logical, interpretable steps. For example, if the agent's initial intent is to ``buy a sofa,'' its plan may involve steps such as searching for sofas, applying filters for delivery date and price, comparing product details, and proceeding to purchase.
These plans are continuously refined as new information becomes available through perception and memory updates.
The generated plan is then stored as a plan memory piece in the Memory Stream, to be retrieved and used by other modules.

Specifically, the module performs three core functions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Initial Plan Creation:} At initialization, the agent formulates an initial plan based on the persona's intent. For example, if the intent is ``buy a sofa,'' the initial plan may involve searching online stores, reviewing search results, and selecting potential products.
    \item \textbf{Plan Updates:} As new observations and memories emerge, the module updates the plan accordingly. For instance, if the agent is browsing search results and finds a sofa that matches the persona's preferences (e.g., color or size), the plan evolves to include reviewing detailed information and considering a purchase.
    \item \textbf{Rationale and Next Step:} To ensure transparency and interpretability, the module provides a clear rationale for its current decisions and explicitly identifies the next step required to achieve the agent's intent, ensuring logical progression toward goals.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Action Module}
\label{sec:action-module}

The Action Module is responsible for executing the agent's planned actions in the web environment. It is designed to mimic how humans translate intentions into interface-level interactions. The module takes as input the current environment state, the agent’s persona and intent, the plan generated by the Planning Module, and relevant memories retrieved from the Memory Stream.

From the current step in the plan (e.g., ``I'm going to search for massage lotion''), the Action Module generates a corresponding low-level action defined in the predefined action space (see Section~\ref{sec:action-space}) as well as it's textual description.
The generated action is executed using the Web Browser Connector and also stored in the Memory Stream as an action memory piece.
For instance, the above plan step may result in an action like \code[breaklines]{type(``massage lotion'', search_input)}.
This design enables the agent to perform realistic, human-like interactions with real web environments, such as typing, clicking, navigating, and submitting forms.

\paragraph{Reflection Module}
\label{sec:reflection-module}
The Reflection Module simulates human reflective thinking by synthesizing recent memories—including observations, actions, previous plans, and reflections. It generates high-level insights that capture key takeaways and strategies,  mimicking how a person would reason in similar situations.
These reflections are stored in the Memory Stream as thought memory pieces, informing and guiding future decisions by the agent.

\paragraph{Wonder Module}
\label{sec:wonder-module}
Humans frequently engage in spontaneous, unrelated thoughts, allowing their minds to drift beyond the current task. To emulate this behavior, the Wonder Module prompts the agent to generate unrelated, spontaneous thoughts or ``wonders'' based on the persona and recent memories. Although these thoughts may not directly impact immediate tasks, they enhance realism in simulated user behavior.
Generated wonders are stored in the Memory Stream as thought memory pieces, becoming accessible to other modules.

\subsubsection{Memory Stream}
\label{sec:memory-stream}

% The \textbf{Memory Stream} is a critical component connecting the Fast Loop and Slow Loop, storing the agent's memories such as observations, actions, reflections, and spontaneous thoughts. 
The \textbf{Memory Stream} stores the agent's memory pieces (including observations, actions, reflections, and spontaneous thoughts) as natural-language descriptions with associated timestamps.
It serves as a critical bridge between the Fast Loop and the Slow Loop and enables these two asynchronous loops to exchange information and remain contextually aligned.
% is a critical component connecting the Fast Loop and Slow Loop, 
% Each memory entry includes a natural-language description along with a creation timestamp.

As memories accumulate, the Memory Stream becomes increasingly lengthy, posing efficiency challenges for memory retrieval.
To address this, we implement a retrieval module that prioritizes and retrieves memories relevant to the agent's current task.
Following prior work \cite{parkGenerativeAgentsInteractive2023}, our retrieval mechanism scores memories based on three dimensions: \textbf{importance}, \textbf{relevance}, and \textbf{recency}. The final retrieval score for each memory is calculated as:
\[
score = (importance \times w_{imp} + relevance \times w_{rel} + recency \times w_{rec}) \times w_{type}
\]
where $w_{imp}$, $w_{rel}$, and $w_{rec}$ represent the weights for importance, relevance, and recency, respectively, and $w_{type}$ is a weight determined by the memory type.
Each module uses separate parameter sets to align with their distinct retrieval priorities: modules in the Fast Loop emphasize recency for rapid actions, while modules in the Slow Loop emphasize relevance for deeper reasoning, mirroring human cognitive patterns.


\paragraph{Importance}
\label{sec:importance}


The importance score measures how closely a memory piece aligns with the agent's original intent. In real-world web environments, users often ignore irrelevant elements like ads or unrelated content. To mimic this behavior and improve simulation efficiency, each memory piece is assigned an importance score by an LLM, helping the agent prioritize task-relevant information (e.g., product listings, prices) over peripheral details. To maintain responsiveness, importance scores are computed only in the Slow Loop; memory pieces without a score are left undefined, and their importance scores are ignored during retrieval.

\paragraph{Relevance}
The relevance score reflects the semantic similarity between a memory and the current context. Each memory's natural-language description is transformed into a vector embedding, and its cosine similarity is calculated against a query string generated by the retrieval module.

\paragraph{Recency}  
The recency score prioritizes recently created memory pieces, as they are more likely to be relevant to the agent’s immediate task. Since our agent focuses on short-term, in-session simulation (as opposed to prior work that models long-term behavior) recency plays a critical role in supporting rapid, context-aware decision-making. It is computed using an exponential decay function based on memory timestamps:
\[
    \text{recency} = \frac{1}{e^{k(t_0 - t)}}
\]
where $t$ is the timestamp of the memory, $t_0$ is the current time, and $k$ is a decay rate constant. A higher $k$ makes the agent focus more heavily on the most recent memories, while a lower $k$ allows for a broader temporal range. Through empirical trial runs, we set $k = 1$ in our implementation.

\subsubsection{Observation Space}
\label{sec:html-parser}
The observation space of the Universal Web Connector is structured as a JSON object containing:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \code{html}: Simplified HTML representation of the webpage.
    \item \code{clickable_elements}: List of clickable elements (e.g., buttons, links).
    \item \code{input_elements}: List of input elements (e.g., text fields, dropdowns).
    \item \code{hoverable_elements}: List of hoverable elements (e.g., hover-triggered menus, tooltips)
    \item \code{select_elements}: List of drop-down single select forms
    \item \code{tabs}: List of currently available browser tabs
    \item \code{error}: Description of errors encountered, such as interacting with a non-existent element.
\end{itemize}


To mimic human perception of a web page, we design a \textbf{DOM (Document Object Model) parser} that reduces the page to elements visible and meaningful to users. Invisible content such as scripts, styles, and metadata is discarded, along with elements hidden by CSS, rendered at zero size, or pushed entirely off-screen. To reduce structural redundancy, the parser collapses trivial nesting of non-semantic containers, such as chains of \code{<div>} wrappers, and prunes empty elements. Exceptions are made for controls that may legitimately appear empty (e.g., \code{<input>}, \code{<img>}, etc.). 

The parser heuristically determines whether elements are interactive. Nodes are marked as clickable if they are native controls (e.g., \code{<button>}, \code{<input>}), anchors with \code{href}, elements with explicit \code{onclick} handlers, or elements with ARIA roles such as \code{button} or \code{link}. Humans can easily distinguish between selectable text (indicated by ``\raisebox{-0.2ex}{\includegraphics[height=1.4ex]{figures/text001.png}}'') and clickable elements (indicated by ``\raisebox{-0.2ex}{\includegraphics[height=1.4ex]{figures/pointer001.png}}''), whereas agents often attempt to click on plain text or other non-interactive elements. To reduce such errors, we expose the same cues available to humans: elements with a computed cursor style of \code{pointer} are considered clickable. To further identify hover-sensitive targets, we monkey-patch \code{addEventListener} so that whenever a node registers a hover event listener, it is annotated with \code{maybe-hoverable=true}.



To enable the model to reference elements during actions, each interactive element is assigned a stable, human-readable identifier derived from visible text, placeholders, or tag names. Identifiers are normalized to short slugs, scoped hierarchically, and made unique with numeric suffixes. Both the stripped node and original DOM share the same \code{semantic-id}, with clickable elements additionally marked \code{clickable=true}. Additionally, since JavaScript states such as input values are not directly available in the HTML, the parser extracts and records them, including values, selections, and focus, and assigns semantic identifiers where needed.

Optionally, when the user wants to evaluate layout-level features, such as comparing two different designs, the observation space can also include a full-page \textbf{screenshot of the webpage}. This provides access to visual cues such as spatial arrangement, alignment, and whitespace that are not recoverable from the stripped DOM alone. By default, the system uses text-only observations, which are more compact and faster to process, while the visual extension offers richer context at the cost of additional computation.

\subsubsection{Action Space}
\label{sec:action-space}

We design the action space so that agents can perform the same primitive interactions as human users (click, type, hover, select, navigate, manage tabs). To reduce latency and eliminate unnecessary branching, we explicitly remove the \emph{scroll} action (the environment automatically scrolls the target element into view before execution). Targets are referenced by the stable semantic identifiers described in the observation construction (\code{semantic-id}), which makes actions robust to minor DOM changes. After each action, the executor waits for network and UI quiescence (for example, no active requests for a short idle window) before returning the next observation. This design addresses prior challenges of noisy action choices, arbitrary code generation, and nondeterministic post-action states by keeping the action set compact, interpretable, and synchronized with the rendered page.

All actions that operate on elements identify their targets using semantic IDs that are guaranteed to be unique within the current observation. Before execution, the environment validates that the target remains interactable, scrolls it into view automatically (therefore, no explicit scroll action is necessary), performs the operation, and then waits for an idle period before emitting the next observation. A full list of available actions can be found in appendix \ref{sec:full-action-space}

In parallel, the reasoning trace viewer shows the agent's internal reasoning, including observations, thoughts, and reflections, which can be expanded for detailed inspection.

The system also supports interactive post-study interviews (Fig.~\ref{fig:interview}).
Researchers can conduct interviews after an LLM agent participant has completed its interaction with the website, allowing them to collect qualitative feedback.
Interviews may also be injected mid-simulation at a specific timestamp.
In this case, the agent responds based only on its memory up to that point, without knowledge of future events, enabling researchers to probe reasoning in real time.
Unlike human participants, whose answers may be influenced by confirmation bias \cite{klayman1995varieties} when recalling earlier steps, LLM agents can be interviewed using a precise memory snapshot at a given moment, providing an accurate window into their decision-making process.  



\section{Case Study: A Usability Testing Simulation with \projectname to Evaluate a Newly Designed Product Filter Feature in an Online Shopping Website }

We chose online shopping, a common and highly personalized use case, to illustrate how UX researchers can use \projectname to augment their usability testing work practice.
In our evaluation study, we used WebArena~\cite{zhouWebArenaRealisticWeb2024} as our target online shopping web environment.
We chose WebArena as our experiment platform because it works similarly to Amazon.com and is a publicly available online shopping environment, widely used in many web agent studies\cite{suLearnbyinteractDataCentricFramework2024, yangAgentOccamSimpleStrong2024}.


\subsection{LLM Agent's Usability Testing Task and Simulated Participant Data}

In our study, we first created an initial usability testing study protocol borrowed from prior HCI works \cite{Moser2017, prakash2024}.
We employed the Persona Generator Module to create 20 agent personas, ensuring a uniform distribution across gender (male, female, and non-binary) and online shopping frequency (a few times per year, a few times per month, and a few times per week). 
LLM agent module uses each persona to perform a usability testing task: \textit{``Buy a mean substitute. With the highest rating and within a budget between \$100 and \$200. You don't need to finish the purchase, just go to the checkout page.''}
LLM agents were free to explore the WebArena shopping environment via multi-turn interaction to test the newly designed feature of ``product filtering panel''~\cite{zhouWebArenaRealisticWeb2024}.  
They may or maynot use the filter naturally, or they could either complete a purchase or terminate the session without purchase, if they could not find a suitable item.
During each session, our system recorded the full simulation result, including the LLM agent participants' reasoning and action traces, as well as their post-study surveys.
Examples of the generated data are shown in appendix \ref{sec:example-data}.

As a reference to the system's scalability, running 20 simulations took approximately 90 minutes, and 1,000 simulations took about 12 hours.
The overall runtime primarily depends on the LLM API's rate limits or token throughput.

\subsection{User Study with 16 UX Researchers}
\label{sec:study-protocol}

We recruited 16 UX researchers to participate in a heuristic evaluation study with reviewing the 20 simulated sessions using \projectname. 
The detailed demographics and experience levels of these UX researcher participants are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:findings}. Five participants reported more than 5 years of UX research experience, two reported 4--5 years, three reported 3--4 years, one reported 2--3 years, five reported 1--2 years, and one reported less than one year. Most participants rated themselves as 3 within 1 to 5 in usability testing familiarity (M=3.87, SD=0.92).

Each study session lasted approximately 30 minutes and was video recorded if they consented. Participants were first presented with the initial draft of the usability testing study design protocol (included in Appendix \ref{sec:study-protocil-draft}); if they are unsatisfied, they are instructed to make suggestions to revise the study design. 
They then used the Study Configure Interface  to config the study design in \projectname. 
To accommodate the time limit, participants were provided with results from a pre-configured run rather than executing new simulations within the 30 mins of study sessoin in real-time. 
Using the Result Viewer Interface, they reviewed and analyzed the generated data of the 20 LLM agent participants. 
UX researcher participants also conducted a post-study interview with the LLM agent, following the interview protocol defined in the draft study design. 

After completing the data analysis and interviewing the agent, UX researchers were asked to propose improvement suggestions both to the initial study protocol and to the new ``filter feature'' design on the shopping website. 
Finally, they completed a post-study survey consisting of Likert-scale items evaluating \projectname's ease of use, the helpfulness and trustworthiness of the generated data, and their satisfaction with the revised protocol. 
We also administered a structured questionnaire, which included items probing how participants think \textit{``LLM agents could replace human participants in the future usability testings'',} as well as open-ended questions to gather their feedback on \projectname's desired new features, additional data modalities, potential risks, and other qualitative feedback. \paragraph{\textbf{UX researcher participants went through a four-step paradigm of using \projectname:}} 1) Building trust with data, 2) sense making of the data, 3) proposing hypotheses and in-depth exploration, and 4) drawing conclusions.

When participants had access to all types of data generated by \projectname, their predominant response was to examine each data type to ensure its trustworthiness. 
Most participants only proceeded with deeper analysis after confirming the data's trustworthiness. However, some participants began to analyze the data early on without fully verifying their trustworthiness and encountered challenges at later stages.
Once trust was established, they moved on to the sense-making phase, where they tried to interpret the data and understand the relationships between different variables.
P3 used the sorting feature to understand different agents' behavior and picked the agent that made the most clicks on the filters to interview to understand why.
Participants then made hypotheses on the experiment result and made detailed observations to verify their hypotheses.
Most participants noticed agents were looking to sort products by their reviews, but couldn't find the system feature. 
They then checked the raw action log and interviewed the agent about their feelings about this.
Participants then discussed what they found during the analysis of the simulated result.
They organized the findings and discussed how they were going to revise and iterate their study design after the simulation.

\paragraph{\textbf{UX Researchers can use the \projectname to gather early feedback from LLM agents and iterate the study design.}}

Before the study, 12 of the 16 participants suggested minor revisions to the initial usability testing protocol, while the remaining participants agreed that the original design was ready for a human pilot study.
The proposed changes included adding additional measurements, increasing the number of participants, and, in some cases, modifying the system’s UI design.
Afterward, all 16 participants were satisfied with the revised study protocol and proceeded to use \projectname.
During each session, the UX researcher participants followed our study protocol, as described in Sec.~\ref{sec:study-protocol}, to review the LLM agent–simulated usability testing data, conduct interviews, and perform analyses.
At the end of the session, each participant also completed a post-study questionnaire with open-ended questions to report their perceived trustworthiness and satisfaction with \projectname.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Design Considerations for  LLM Agent Systems in User Study}
\label{sec:discussion-design-considerations}

The shift towards using LLMs to assist UX research has demonstrated considerable potential. With \projectname, we show that LLM agents can support user studies by enabling faster design iterations through user behavior simulation, thereby reducing costs and improving the quality of study design~\cite{decker-maurerMethodMadnessUsability2012}. Building on these findings, we outline several design considerations to guide the development of future systems involving AI-simulated agents in user studies.  

\paragraph{Generate high-level insights from raw data.}  
Future systems should help researchers move beyond raw reasoning traces by automatically generating high-level insights. As P5 noted, visualizations beyond simple aggregate measures (e.g., mean values) would make it easier to intuitively identify issues. Similarly, P3 reported feeling overwhelmed by the detail page, suggesting that concise summaries of memory data would improve usability.  

\paragraph{Support more modalities of simulation and result.}  
Future systems should extend beyond text-based interaction and static outputs~\cite{liu2025personaflow, kuangCollaborationConversationalAI2023}. P3 suggested enabling LLM agents to interact with systems through voice and tone, thereby broadening applicability to domains such as conversational interfaces. P4 recommended incorporating video replays in addition to step-by-step traces to better capture the dynamics of interactions. P2 further emphasized the need to expand beyond web environments to include graphical user interfaces. Together, these suggestions highlight the importance of supporting multimodal simulation and diverse result formats to improve both ecological validity and researcher insight.  

\subsection{Potential Downstream Application for LLM Agent Systems}
In this work, we explored the use of LLM Agents to simulate human participants in the context of usability testing. However, the underlying capabilities of these agents—such as reasoning, goal-driven behavior, and interaction with web environments—open the door to a variety of downstream applications beyond UX research.

\paragraph{Help with Low-Resource UX Research}  
Although LLM agent simulations cannot yet replace human participants, they can be a valuable resource in low-resource research settings. In many educational contexts, students conducting usability studies often rely on friends or family members as participants due to limited access to target users~\cite{houde2025controlling, wang2025user}. A system like \projectname could help by providing simulated participants that approximate realistic user behaviors, allowing students to practice study design, iterate on protocols, and identify usability issues before recruiting actual participants. Similarly, independent researchers or practitioners with limited budgets could use simulated agents to pilot studies quickly and cost-effectively, reducing reliance on extensive recruitment and compensation~\cite{kapania2025simulacrum}. While these simulations are not substitutes for human data~\cite{ashkinaze2025plurals}, they can significantly lower the barrier to entry for conducting UX research and provide a practical first step in the iterative design process.  


\paragraph{Digital Twin for End-User Task Automation}
LLM Agents can serve as ``digital twins'', acting on behalf of users to complete tasks in web environments~\cite{vahdati2025multi}. For example, in online shopping scenarios, a future system could allow users to specify their preferences and intents (e.g., budget, brand, or product features), and delegate the search, comparison, and purchase decisions to a personalized agent. This application would enable users to offload routine decision-making while still aligning outcomes with their goals.


\paragraph{Evaluation of Existing AI Systems}

Simulated users can also serve as proxies for evaluating existing AI systems, such as recommender systems and search engines~\cite{ma2025pub, zhao2025exploring}. Unlike traditional evaluation methods that rely on historical interaction logs or static benchmarks, LLM Agents can dynamically engage with system outputs, simulate realistic user behavior, and provide feedback. By assigning diverse personas to the agents, researchers can examine how users with different goals, preferences, or constraints interact with AI systems. This simulation-based approach offers a richer, more nuanced perspective on system performance, fairness, and user alignment across varied use cases.

\paragraph{Simulating A/B Testing with Behavioral Feedback}
LLM Agents can be deployed to simulate A/B tests for different interfaces and model variants, not only measuring which version performs better in terms of task success but also collecting rich behavioral data and simulated feedback.
This enables researchers to evaluate both the quantitative and qualitative impact of design changes before allocating real A/B testing resources, potentially reducing iteration cycles and development costs.

\subsection{Resolving Risk and Privacy Concerns}
% \note{[maybe remove]}

While LLM-assisted UX study systems offer substantial benefits for user studies, they also introduce significant risks and concerns, particularly around privacy and ethical use \cite{liHumanCenteredPrivacyResearch2024, shaoPrivacyLensEvaluatingPrivacy2024}. 
A notable concern about sensitive data might arise in applying LLM Agents in user studies in fields such as healthcare \cite{perisPrivacyTimeLanguage2023}.
Our participants viewed data from LLM Agents as supplementary rather than definitive, which is consistent with prior findings that LLMs cannot fully replace human subjects in psychological experiments~\cite{cui2024can}. 
This preference reflects their ability to detect biases, limiting acceptance of LLMs as accurate representations of outcomes~\cite{schmidtSimulatingHumanHCD2024}.

Another critical issue is the risk of researchers misusing AI-generated data \cite{perisPrivacyTimeLanguage2023}. 
Some may be tempted to use this simulated data as a substitute for actual human participant data, potentially bypassing real user studies altogether. 
This could lead to flawed conclusions, as the AI-generated data, while useful for simulations, cannot fully replicate the complexity of real human behavior.
We believe that addressing these risks requires a clear understanding of the limitations of LLM-generated data and establishing rigorous ethical guidelines and robust privacy safeguards to ensure responsible and effective integration of LLM in UX research.

% Finally, there are broader concerns about AI replacing human participants in research entirely. While LLM Agent simulations can enhance study design and simulate certain behaviors, they come with inherent bias and should not replace the insights gained from real human participants. It is crucial to maintain a balance where AI assists but does not replace human involvement in research.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Works}
There are several limitations in our work. 
First, the limited number of participants in our heuristic evaluation may not fully capture the diversity of user experiences or the generalizability of the findings. 
A larger scale of rigorous user testing in the future will help us further explore the effectiveness of \projectname.
Also, our current work focuses only on the qualitative analysis of the participants' perspectives toward LLM Agent simulation, but not on quantitative evaluations of the LLM Agents' versus real humans' shopping behaviors. 
It is crucial to conduct a systematic analysis comparing LLM-generated simulations with real human participants in order to further understand how UX researchers could benefit from using such agents.

Our current results confirm the effectiveness of \projectname in web-related user research, as well as the potential of LLMs in simulating user behavior to assist rapid iteration of user research. 
In the future, this work could be expanded to encompass a broader range of user research contexts beyond web applications. 
This might include adaptation to UI/UX design in desktop, mobile, or mixed-reality environments, where LLM Agents could simulate user interactions in complex, multi-modal interfaces. 
Also, we only tested giving the agents an explicit intention of ``buy a jacket''. Experimenting with other intents, such as window shopping, can also help understand the ability and limits of LLM Agent.
Additionally, the action space of the agent is currently limited to basic actions such as ``click'' and ``type''. Supporting other types of actions, such as ``wait'', ``read'', and ``scroll'' can allow the experimenter to collect more information useful to their study.

\section{Conclusion}

% \note{one paragraph}
In this work, we present \projectname to support researchers in conducting simulated user studies.
By providing action traces, reasoning traces, and post-study survey results, the system supports iterative refinement of both UX study designs and feature designs.
Our case study with 16 UX researcher participants suggests that \textbf{\projectname helps them identify potential flaws in study protocol } and gains early insights into how users might perceive and interact with the proposed web feature, thus improving the quality and reducing risks of the later experiments with real human participants.
Our study is the first step towards a promising future of UX researchers to use LLM agents to achieve ``human-AI collaboration'' in the field of UX research.

\section{Study Protocol Draft}
\label{sec:study-protocil-draft}
\section*{Study Context}
You are the designer of a shopping website and have just completed the implementation of a new ``left filter panel'' on the search results page.

\section*{Draft Study Protocol}
The goal of this study is to evaluate the usability and effectiveness of the left filter panel in supporting users' search and purchase tasks. 

\subsection*{Study Procedure}
Participants are asked to:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Finish the participant task on the shopping website
    \item Finish a survey questionnaire
    \item Finish a post-study interview conducted by you
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Measurements}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Click count: total number of clicks made by the user
    \item Filter click count: total number of clicks on the filters
    \item Step count: total number of steps taken to complete the task
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Participant Task}
Buy the highest rated product from the meat substitute category within a budget between 100 and 200. You don't need to finish the purchase, just go to the checkout page.

\subsection*{Participants}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Total: 20 participants
    \item Age range: 18--55 
    \item Gender: Male, Female, Non-binary (1:1:1)
    \item Online Shopping Frequency: evenly distributed across three frequency groups (1:1:1)
    \begin{itemize}
        \item A few times per year
        \item A few times per month
        \item A few times per week
    \end{itemize}
